Unit 3 — Introduction to 3D-IC Packaging

What is a 3D-IC?

A 3-Dimensional Integrated Circuit (3D-IC) is a semiconductor architecture where multiple silicon wafers or dies are stacked vertically and interconnected using Through-Silicon Vias (TSVs). Unlike 2D (side-by-side) or 2.5D (on interposer) packaging, 3D-IC uses the Z-axis — reducing average interconnect length, lowering parasitic resistance (R), capacitance (C), and inductance (L).

3D-IC (TSV-based)

  • Separate, pre-fabricated dies stacked together
  • Interconnected via TSVs (micron-scale diameter)
  • "System-in-Package" — Macro-integration level
  • Example: HBM stacks, Intel Foveros

Monolithic 3D (M3D)

  • Layers built sequentially on the same wafer
  • L0 fabricated → Si deposited → L1 fabricated above
  • Connected via MIVs (nanometer-scale — like standard vias)
  • "Transistor-on-Transistor" — Nano-integration level

A. Interconnect Bottleneck (RC Delay)

In complex 2D designs, signals travel long distances across substrate/PCB. Long wires → high resistance (R) and capacitance (C) → high RC delay → limits speed and wastes power.

Delay τ ∝ R × C ∝ Wire_Length² Stacking: d_z (vertical) ≪ d_x,y (horizontal)

3D stacking reduces the signal path from millimeters to micrometers.

B. Memory Bandwidth Wall

Modern CPUs/GPUs need massive memory bandwidth but side-by-side connections limit parallel I/O pins. This is the "Memory Wall" — high latency from off-chip DRAM access.

HBM Solution: Stack DRAM dies vertically on logic die via TSVs → >1024-bit bus, near-zero latency, 500+ GB/s bandwidth.

C. Power Consumption

Shorter interconnects mean lower capacitance to charge/discharge each cycle, reducing dynamic power:

P ≈ C_load × V² × f Shorter wire → less C_load → 25–40% less power consumption

Types of Chiplet Packaging

2D Packaging

Chiplets placed side-by-side on a common substrate. Coarser interconnects; simpler manufacturing.

Example: Basic multi-chip modules

2.5D Packaging

Chiplets on a silicon interposer that provides high-density horizontal RDL wiring. No direct die stacking.

Example: AMD EPYC, NVIDIA A100

3D Packaging

Chiplets stacked vertically with TSVs for inter-die connection. Highest density, shortest paths.

Example: HBM, Intel Foveros

TSV Physical Structure (Compact Model)

TSV Cross-Section — 3 Key Layers

1

Copper Fill (Core): Main conductor — electroplated Cu. Carries primary current. Has resistivity ρ = 1.7×10⁻⁸ Ω·m.

2

Dielectric Isolation Liner: SiO₂ barrier between Cu and Si. Prevents Cu diffusion. Thickness determines parasitic capacitance (C_TSV).

3

Silicon Bulk: Semi-conductive surrounding medium. Introduces substrate loss. TSVs couple to neighbors through this lossy Si bulk.

Parasitic Electrical Model

R_TSV = ρ × L / A (DC resistance) C_TSV = 2πεL / ln(r_out/r_in) (capacitance) L_TSV = μ₀L/(2π) × ln(r_out/r_in) (inductance) Signal delay: τ = 0.5 × R_TSV × C_TSV
TSV is NOT a simple wire — it is a frequency-dependent coaxial conductor. Behavior changes significantly from 1 GHz to 10 GHz (skin effect).

RDL — Redistribution Layer Functions

Pitch Translation

Chip pad pitch: ~10–30 μm → Interposer: ~50–100 μm → Substrate: 100–200 μm. Gradual fan-out of signals.

Signal Routing

High-density horizontal pathways between chiplets (e.g., CPU ↔ HBM). Multiple metal layers with dielectric insulation.

Power/Ground Planes

Dedicated Cu planes for uniform power distribution. Minimizes IR drop (V = I×R) across the chip.

Working Mechanism of 3D-IC

TSV Fabrication Process — Step by Step

1

Via Etch: Deep holes etched into silicon wafer using DRIE (Deep Reactive Ion Etching). Creates high-aspect-ratio vertical channels.

2

Liner Deposition: Thin SiO₂ isolation liner deposited on via walls. Electrically isolates the Cu fill from Si substrate.

3

Seed Layer + Cu Fill: Thin seed layer deposited, then Cu electroplated to fill the via. Excess Cu removed by CMP (Chemical Mechanical Polishing).

4

Wafer Thinning (Backgrind): Wafer backside is thinned to expose the TSV tips. Brings TSV depth to final target (typically 50–100 μm).

5

Die Alignment & Bonding: Dies precisely aligned and bonded using micro-bumps (solder) or Hybrid Bonding (direct Cu-to-Cu, no solder, sub-10μm pitch).

6

Signal Operation: Electrons flow vertically through the Cu-filled TSV stack from die to die. Delay governed by the RC product of the TSV.

3D-IC as a Problem Solver

A. Interconnect Bottleneck (RC Delay)

In monolithic 2D chips, global wires grow longer as the chip grows — unavoidable in pure scaling. This raises both R (resistance) and C (capacitance) of the wire.

τ_RC = R_wire × C_wire ∝ L² (delay scales with SQUARE of length!) 2D: signal path = 10 mm → τ very large 3D: signal path = 50 μm → τ reduced 40,000× (proportional to L²)
Physical rule: In 3D, d_z (vertical) ≪ d_x,y (horizontal distance in 2D). Blocks that were mm apart are now μm apart.

B. Memory Wall

High-performance computing is bottlenecked by fetching data from off-chip DRAM — the "Memory Wall." This is caused by limited I/O pins and high latency of long wires.

Traditional DDR (2D)

  • 64-bit wide memory bus
  • ~100 ns access latency
  • ~50 GB/s peak bandwidth
  • Separate package, long PCB traces

HBM 3D Stacking

  • >1024-bit wide bus
  • Near-zero latency
  • 500–1000+ GB/s bandwidth
  • Stacked directly on logic die via TSVs

Real-world use: AMD Instinct MI300X, NVIDIA H100, Google TPU v4, Apple M-series all use HBM or stacked memory

C. Power Scaling

Communication over long wires dissipates energy every clock cycle. The power dissipated by a wire:

P_interconnect = A × C_load × V² × f Shorter wire → C_load ↓ → P_interconnect ↓ 3D-IC achieves 25–40% total system power reduction vs equivalent 2D design
Every 2× reduction in wire length → ~4× reduction in wire capacitance → significant power savings!

D. Form Factor

Stacking allows more functionality in a smaller package footprint, essential for mobile devices and dense computing systems. Instead of a large 2D chip, multiple smaller dies are stacked vertically in the same XY area.

Example: Smartphone application processors use PoP stacking — the logic chip and LPDDR memory are stacked in a package barely larger than a postage stamp.

3D-ICs as Homogeneous Assemblies

What is a Homogeneous 3D-IC?

A Homogeneous 3D-IC consists of multiple identical dies stacked together. The most common example is HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) — 4 to 12 layers of the exact same DRAM die, stacked vertically and interconnected via TSVs.

Advantages in Detail

1

Yield Advantage: Manufacturing yield drops exponentially with die area. It is far easier to produce 8 small, high-yield memory dies than one massive monolithic memory chip of equivalent total capacity. Each small die has far fewer defects.

2

Linear Scalability: Performance or storage capacity scales linearly by adding more layers. HBM comes in 4-Hi, 8-Hi, 12-Hi stack configurations. Each additional layer adds directly to total bandwidth and capacity.

3

Redundancy via LoB: A Logic-on-Base (LoB) controller die at the stack base can reconfigure the stack to bypass a faulty die layer. Advanced error correction (ECC) is also embedded within the stack.

Challenges: Thermal Management in 3D-ICs

Thermal management is the primary physical constraint preventing unlimited 3D stacking.

A. Power Density Concentration

In 2D: heat dissipates through one top surface area. In 3D: each stacked layer generates power, but the heatsink footprint (XY area) stays the same as a single die.

Total Power: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + ... + Pₙ = Σ Pᵢ Example: 4 layers, each 10W → P_total = 40W But cooling area is the same as for ONE 10W die! Power density QUADRUPLES while cooling area stays constant.

B. Vertical Thermal Resistance (Rθ)

Heat from the bottom die must pass through multiple layers of silicon, inter-layer dielectric (ILD), and bonding adhesives before reaching the heatsink. Each layer adds thermal resistance.

T_j = T_ambient + P × (R_pkg + R_die + R_bond) Thermal conductivity: Si: 150 W/mK ← good conductor Cu (TSV): 400 W/mK ← excellent conductor Underfill/BCB: 0.2 W/mK ← very poor! thermal insulator
Bonding layers (underfill/adhesive) act as thermal insulators (k ≈ 0.2 W/mK). They trap heat between dies — a major design challenge.

C. Thermal Crosstalk

A localized "Hot Spot" in the bottom logic die generates heat that conducts vertically upward — heating the memory die stacked above. This causes timing failures in the memory circuits (temperature changes propagation delay).

Problem

Hot spot in logic die → heat conducts up → memory die temperature rises → transistor threshold voltage shifts → timing violation (setup/hold)

Solution: Thermal-Aware Floorplanning

High-power logic blocks are physically offset between die layers, so hot spots in one die do not directly align with timing-critical circuits in adjacent die layers.

D. Fourier's Law & Thermal Runaway

Fourier's Law of Heat Flux: q = -k × ∇T q = heat flux (W/m²) k = thermal conductivity (W/mK) ∇T = temperature gradient (K/m) In 3D-IC: if k is low AND q is high → ∇T becomes VERY large
Thermal Runaway Mechanism:
High Temperature → Higher leakage (I_leak ∝ e^(-Eg/kT)) → More heat generated → Even higher temperature → Runaway failure!

Stress Analysis & Power Delivery Network

The Piezoresistive Effect — Stress as an Electrical Problem

In 3D-IC, stress is NOT just a mechanical failure risk — it is an electrical performance variable. Silicon is a piezoresistive material: when its crystal lattice is strained (by a Cu TSV with different CTE than Si), the energy bands shift, changing the effective mass of charge carriers.

Compressive Stress — Radial (σ_rr)

  • Acts inward toward the TSV
  • Increases hole mobility (μₚ) — good for PMOS
  • May decrease electron mobility (μₙ) — bad for NMOS
  • Effect depends on crystal orientation ([100] vs [110])

Tensile Stress — Tangential (σ_θθ)

  • Acts circumferentially around TSV
  • Opposite sign to radial stress
  • Different mobility impact on n-type vs p-type
  • Must analyze separately for each transistor type

Keep-Out Zone (KOZ)

Mobility change formula: Δμ/μ = π_r × σ_r π_r = piezoresistive coefficient (Pa⁻¹) σ_r = radial stress at distance r from TSV center Typical: Δμ/μ can reach ~10% near TSV

Because mobility changes up to 10%, transistors near a TSV switch at different speeds than designed — causing timing skew (setup/hold violations). The KOZ is the exclusion zone where no timing-critical transistors are placed.

Analogy: Like expansion joints in a bridge — the joint (KOZ) exists because the metal (Cu TSV) expands much more than the concrete (Si) when heated.

Lamé Equations — Mathematical Stress Distribution

The TSV is modeled as a cylindrical Cu inclusion in an infinite Si matrix. The thermal stress at radial distance r from TSV center:

Radial stress (Compressive): σ_rr(r) = -(E × Δα × ΔT / 2) × (R/r)² Tangential stress (Tensile): σ_θθ(r) = +(E × Δα × ΔT / 2) × (R/r)² Where: E = Young's modulus of Si (~130 GPa) Δα = CTE mismatch = α_Cu - α_Si = 17 - 2.3 = 14.7 ppm/°C ΔT = Temperature change from stress-free state R = TSV radius r = distance from TSV center (r ≥ R)
Critical: Stress decays as 1/r². Doubling the distance from TSV center reduces stress impact by 4×. This directly determines the required KOZ radius.

Power Delivery Network (PDN) — Inductive Noise

The PDN distributes VDD and GND through the 3D stack via TSVs. The key challenge is inductive voltage drop (ΔV) when circuits switch rapidly:

V_noise = L × (dI/dt) = L × ΔI / Δt L = TSV path inductance (typically 5–20 pH range) dI = current step dt = switching time (picoseconds in modern circuits)
If supply voltage = 1.0V and V_noise = 0.5V → 50% of supply is lost to noise — catastrophic for circuit timing and reliability!

Solution: On-Chip Decoupling Capacitors

Decap cells placed close to switching circuits provide charge locally — reducing the instantaneous dI/dt demand on the TSV path and suppressing V_noise. Mandatory in every 3D-IC PDN design.

Testability Challenge in 3D-ICs

Once dies are stacked, individual dies cannot be probed externally. Like a strand of Christmas lights — if one "bulb" (die) fails, you cannot easily isolate which one it is.

Solved Numerical Problems

Problem 1 — TSV Resistance Calculation

Given: Cu TSV: diameter = 10 μm, length = 60 μm. Resistivity of Cu: ρ = 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω·m. Find resistance of one TSV.

1

Find cross-sectional area:

r = d/2 = 10/2 = 5 μm = 5 × 10⁻⁶ m A = π × r² = π × (5 × 10⁻⁶)² = π × 25 × 10⁻¹² = 78.5 × 10⁻¹² m²
2

Apply R = ρL/A:

R = (1.7 × 10⁻⁸) × (60 × 10⁻⁶) / (78.5 × 10⁻¹²) R = (1.02 × 10⁻¹²) / (78.5 × 10⁻¹²) R ≈ 0.013 Ω = 13 mΩ
Result: R_TSV ≈ 13 mΩ. Very low resistance confirms Cu TSVs are excellent conductors for short vertical interconnects.

Problem 2 — Thermal Stacking (Temperature Calculation)

Given: 2-layer 3D stack. Die 1 (Bottom) = 10W, Die 2 (Top) = 10W. Rθ (Die2→Die1) = 0.5°C/W. Rθ (Die1→Heatsink) = 0.2°C/W. T_Heatsink = 40°C. Find T of Die 2.

1

ΔT from Die 2 to Die 1 (only Die 2's power flows through this resistance):

ΔT₂→₁ = P_Die2 × Rθ(2→1) = 10 W × 0.5 °C/W = 5°C
2

Total power flowing through Rθ (Die1→Heatsink) = both dies' power combined:

P_total = P_Die1 + P_Die2 = 10 + 10 = 20 W ΔT₁→Sink = 20 W × 0.2 °C/W = 4°C
3

T_Die2 = sum all ΔT from heatsink upward:

T_Die2 = T_Sink + ΔT₁→Sink + ΔT₂→₁ T_Die2 = 40 + 4 + 5 = 49°C
Result: T_Die2 = 49°C. Note: the bottom die's power also heats the top die, since all heat flows through the same thermal path to the heatsink.

Problem 3 — Piezoresistive Impact (Mobility Change)

Given: PMOS transistor placed 10 μm from TSV center. Radial stress σ_r = 150 MPa. Piezoresistive coefficient for holes: π_r = 6.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ Pa⁻¹. Calculate Δμ/μ (percentage mobility change).

1

Convert stress to SI units:

σ_r = 150 MPa = 150 × 10⁶ Pa
2

Apply piezoresistive formula:

Δμ/μ = π_r × σ_r Δμ/μ = (6.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ Pa⁻¹) × (150 × 10⁶ Pa) Δμ/μ = 6.6 × 150 × 10⁻⁴ = 990 × 10⁻⁴ = 0.099
3

Convert to percentage:

Δμ/μ ≈ 9.9% change in hole mobility
Teaching point: ~10% mobility change causes timing violations (setup/hold failures). This MUST be compensated in design via KOZ enforcement — no timing-critical transistors within the stress zone of TSVs.

Problem 4 — PDN Inductive Voltage Drop

Given: 3D-IC top die switches 5A of current in 100 ps. Total TSV path inductance L = 10 pH. Calculate V_noise (inductive voltage drop).

1

Write the inductive voltage formula:

V_noise = L × (dI/dt)
2

Calculate rate of current change:

dI/dt = ΔI / Δt = 5 A / (100 × 10⁻¹² s) = 5 × 10¹⁰ A/s
3

Compute voltage drop:

V_noise = L × (dI/dt) V_noise = (10 × 10⁻¹² H) × (5 × 10¹⁰ A/s) V_noise = 10 × 0.05 = 0.5 V
Teaching point: Supply = 1.0V but V_noise = 0.5V → 50% of supply rail lost to inductive noise! This is why on-chip decoupling capacitors (decaps) are MANDATORY in every 3D-IC PDN design. Without decaps, circuits fail to operate correctly.
