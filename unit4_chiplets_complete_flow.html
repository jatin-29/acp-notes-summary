Topic 1 — ML & AI: Condition Monitoring & Degradation Analysis
Using Machine Learning & AI to detect faults & predict equipment degradation before it fails
Core Definitions
TermDefinitionAnalogy
Condition MonitoringThe process of observing a machine's state over time to detect changes that might indicate an impending failureDoctor taking regular checkups to catch illness early
AI-based Degradation AnalysisUsing AI algorithms to analyze data trends and predict when equipment will deteriorate to a critical levelWeather forecast — not just today's weather but predicting next week's storm
Anomaly DetectionIdentifying unusual sensor patterns that deviate from established normal operating conditionsSecurity camera flagging unusual movement at 3am
Fault DiagnosisClassifying detected anomalies into specific fault types (e.g., bearing fault, gear wear, electrical issue)Doctor not just saying "something's wrong" but diagnosing the exact disease
RUL PredictionRemaining Useful Life — estimating how much longer a component can operate before it is likely to failFuel gauge showing how many km left before the tank is empty
Types of Data Collected in Condition Monitoring
Vibration data
Detects bearing wear, misalignment, or imbalance from changes in vibration patterns
Temperature data
Elevated temps indicate excessive friction, electrical problems, or cooling system issues
Acoustic data
Changes in sound signal leaks, cavitation, or changes in operational noise
Lubricant analysis
Oil/grease testing identifies wear particles, contaminants, or viscosity changes
Electrical data
Monitors current, voltage & power consumption to detect electrical faults
Process parameters
Pressure, flow rate & speed from control systems indicate deviations from normal operation
ML in Condition Monitoring — 3 Key Tasks
TaskDefinitionTechnique Used
Anomaly DetectionFind unusual patterns in sensor data deviating from normalClustering, statistical models, neural networks on historical data
Fault DiagnosisClassify anomalies into specific fault types (bearing fault, gear wear, etc.)Supervised learning trained on labeled fault data
RUL PredictionEstimate how much longer a component can operate before failureRegression models and time-series analysis
AI in Degradation Analysis — 4 Key Roles
RoleDefinition
Identifying Degradation PatternsAnalyze historical & real-time data to find trends showing gradual deterioration — rate of sensor change, anomaly frequency, vibration spectra evolution
Predicting Future DegradationBased on identified patterns, forecast when equipment will reach a critical level requiring maintenance — moves from reactive to truly predictive maintenance
Root Cause AnalysisAnalyze correlations between different sensor streams & operational parameters to pinpoint underlying causes of degradation
Optimizing Maintenance StrategiesInform decisions on when and how to perform maintenance based on predicted degradation & RUL — minimize downtime and costs

Topic 2 — Reliability Physics: Life-Consuming Monitoring & RP-based Damage Accumulation
Understanding WHY components fail physically & estimating how much damage has accumulated
Core Definitions
TermDefinitionAnalogy
Reliability Physics (Physics of Failure)An engineering discipline that models the physical, chemical & mechanical processes that CAUSE failure in materials, components & systems — asks WHY things fail, not just WHENInstead of just noting a car breaks down every 5 years, understanding that metal fatigue in the engine block is the root cause
Life-Consuming MonitoringOperational monitoring focused on measuring the stressors & usage that directly consume a component's life — based on reliability physics principlesCar's odometer tracking km driven — each km "consumes" engine life based on physics of wear
RP-based Damage Accumulation EstimationUsing Reliability Physics models to mathematically estimate how much damage has accumulated inside a component based on measured stressors — since we cannot directly SEE damage growing insideEstimating how much a road has deteriorated based on traffic load data — you don't dig it up to check, you calculate from physics models
Reliability Physics — Key Aspects
AspectDefinition
Identifying Failure MechanismsFatigue crack growth, corrosion, wear, dielectric breakdown — the specific physical processes causing failure
Understanding StressesThermal, mechanical, electrical & chemical stresses that drive these failure mechanisms
Developing Physics ModelsMathematical models relating stress, material properties & time to the progression of damage
GoalPredict product life based on design, materials, manufacturing & expected operating environment — BEFORE extensive testing or field data is available
Life-Consuming Monitoring — What Gets Monitored
Load Cycles
For fatigue — e.g. number of stress cycles on a bridge or aircraft wing
Temperature Profiles
For thermal degradation — e.g. temperature cycles on electronic components
Vibration Intensity & Duration
For wear or structural fatigue — how hard and how long vibration occurs
Corrosive Environment Exposure
For chemical degradation — how long and how intensely exposed to corrosive agents
Operating Hours
Under specific conditions for general wear-out tracking
Connection to RP
Life is "consumed" per the physics of dominant failure mechanisms — monitoring these tracks how much inherent life has been used up
RP-based Damage Accumulation Estimation — How It Works
ComponentDefinition
Damage MetricThe measurable sign of damage — e.g. crack length, material loss, insulation resistance change — modeled as a function of life-consuming stressors
Physics ModelMathematical description of how damage metric progresses based on the stressors — involves material properties & physical laws of the failure mechanism
Kalman FilterA filtering technique used to combine physics model predictions with noisy sensor data — improves accuracy of damage estimation
OutcomeA continuously updated estimate of the current damage state or remaining life of the specific component being monitored

Topic 3 — PHM, Degradation & Failure Mechanisms
The complete discipline — monitoring degradation, understanding failure mechanisms & predicting remaining life
3 Core Concepts — Definitions
ConceptDefinitionThink of it as
DegradationThe process by which the performance, condition or integrity of a system, component or material deteriorates over time and/or usageA gradual decline in health or capability — the STATE of getting worse
Failure MechanismThe specific physical, chemical or other process that CAUSES degradation and ultimately leads to failure — it is the REASON for the degradationThe "how" or "why" behind the deterioration
PHM (Prognostics & Health Management)An engineering discipline focused on assessing the current health of a system & predicting its future health and Remaining Useful Life (RUL)Like a doctor's prognosis — not just diagnosing what is wrong now but predicting what will go wrong later
Degradation — Examples & Key Points
Examples of Degradation
A bearing becoming rougher · A crack growing in a structure · A battery losing charge capacity · Insulation losing dielectric strength
Key Point
Degradation is often continuous BEFORE complete failure — it shows up as reduced efficiency, increased noise, higher temperatures, or changed vibration patterns
Failure Mechanisms — Types & Importance
Failure MechanismDefinitionExample
FatigueRepeated stress cycles causing cracks to initiate and growAircraft wing cracking after millions of flight cycles
WearSurface material being removed due to friction between surfacesBrake pads thinning after repeated braking
CorrosionChemical or electrochemical reaction with environment causing material deteriorationIron rusting when exposed to oxygen & moisture
CreepDeformation under sustained stress at elevated temperaturesMetal turbine blades slowly elongating under high heat
Electrical BreakdownLoss of insulating properties in electrical componentsChip oxide layer failing under high voltage stress (TDDB)
Why Identifying Failure Mechanisms Matters
Design more reliable components
Mitigate the mechanism at design stage
Develop effective monitoring
Look for signs of the specific failure mechanism
Model & predict degradation rate
Use physics to forecast when failure will occur
PHM — Goal & Maintenance Approach Evolution
ApproachDefinitionProblem
Reactive Maintenance"Fix it when it breaks" — wait for failure then repairUnplanned downtime, safety risks, expensive emergency repairs
Preventive Maintenance"Fix it on a schedule" — replace parts at fixed intervals regardless of conditionWasteful — sometimes parts replaced too early, sometimes too late
Predictive Maintenance (PHM Goal)"Fix it just before it is likely to break" — predict failure using data & modelsBest approach — minimizes cost, downtime & waste
PHM — 4 Key Activities
ActivityDefinitionSimple Question it Answers
Condition MonitoringCollecting data on the system's operation and condition continuouslyWhat is the system doing right now?
DiagnosticsIdentifying the current fault or degradation state from the collected dataWhat is wrong RIGHT NOW?
PrognosticsPredicting the future state of health & estimating Remaining Useful Life (RUL)What is likely to go wrong LATER and WHEN?
Health ManagementUsing diagnostic & prognostic information to make informed decisions about maintenance, operation & logisticsWhat should we DO about it?
How PHM, Degradation & Failure Mechanisms Connect
PHM monitors degradation
Continuously watches for signs that the system is getting worse over time
Uses failure mechanism knowledge
Interprets data & builds models to predict how fast degradation will progress under future conditions
RUL = damage threshold
Predicts when accumulated degradation driven by the failure mechanism will reach a critical level causing failure
